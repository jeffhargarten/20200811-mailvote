Amid continuing pandemic conditions, absentee and mail balloting has become a new normal, with surges in early voting providing possible previews of November's general election trends.

It also helped drive the highest primary turnout for Minneapolis in five decades, with two-thirds of the city's ballots being cast early (and less than 1% were spoiled).

Minnesotans have used no-excuse mail and in-person early voting since 2014, which has steadily increased in popularity, allowing for a simpler transition into coronavirus-era elections.

And Minnesotans requested more absentee ballots this year than 2016's entire state primary vote, which helped triple overall turnout compared to the last presidential cycle.

Many Twin Cities metro area counties saw most of their primary vote arrive absentee, despite mail-in balloting traditionally being more common in rural areas. Only about 5% of the early vote was in-person.

Strained operations at the United States Postal Service have sparked nationwide political controversy and congressional action around delayed results and possibly lost absentee ballots in upcoming November contests.

Even with a two-day extension beyond primary day this year, at least a quarter of requested early ballots weren't counted, with some remaining unreturned, arriving late or being rejected for various reasons.

The general election on November 3 will likely test Minnesota's capacity to handle influxes of voters mailing-in their ballots as absentee voting takes on crucial significance.
